
# Between Here and There

## A spring Sunday

I want to tell the story of my trip with **a little more than two and a half grams of mushrooms**, taken on a Sunday morning, **March 21st**, in Zurich, right when spring was beginning.

Even that felt strange to me. As if it wasn’t just a coincidence that I had decided, almost spontaneously, to do it on the exact day spring starts. There was already a kind of **meta-trip** inside the trip, this feeling that intense experiences always come wrapped in some symbolic layer, as if they choose their own timing.

And one of the first things I understood was that when human beings go through very intense experiences, of any kind, the hardest part afterward is **sharing them**.

Not because they aren’t real.

But because they are too real, and too strange, and too personal at the same time.

## The beginning: patterns, music, the scarf

At first I was in that almost obsessive phase where I was **looking for visuals**. I stared at the ceiling, trying to find patterns, wanting to see something. At first I didn’t find much.

Then slowly, with the **music**, something opened up. I started finding these patterns, feeling them, entering them. A kind of joy came over me, very simple, very physical.

At some point I even got up to hang this **psychedelic scarf**, and it made me laugh so much. I basically risked my life on a chair just to hang a scarf, and even now I look at it and think it’s hanging badly, but in that moment it felt incredibly important.

Up until then the trip was **beautiful**, even fun. Just lying there looking at the ceiling felt amazing.

But then it became more intense.

## The mask and total darkness

At a certain point I got tired and decided to put on an **eye mask**. I wanted to close myself into complete darkness.

What bothered me was the difference between real darkness and just closing your eyes, when some light still passes through your eyelids. I wanted **total darkness**.

At first there were the usual psychedelic patterns, more or less strange. But very quickly it stopped being just “I’m seeing things.”

It became something else.

And this is already where it gets hard to explain, because it didn’t happen in a neat sequence. There wasn’t a before and after, no clear moment where one thing ended and another began. It was as if **everything was happening at once**.

There was one part of me living it all, and another part observing and thinking at the same time.

## Here and there

The most important thing in the trip, the one thing I felt I had to remember, was this: there existed **a here and a there**.

In my mind they had become two distinct worlds.

The **here** was this other world that, I felt, wasn’t accessible just to me. It wasn’t some private invention of mine. It was a place where **other human beings had already been**. A place I had probably entered before, with other experiences, with mushrooms, maybe LSD, maybe ayahuasca. And when I entered it again I had a very strong feeling: **I’ve already been here**.

As if I were picking up right where I had left off last time.

The **there**, instead, was the normal world, everyday reality, the one you return to and have to use linear words to explain something that is not linear at all.

In the world of here there were **truths** that you didn’t just understand intellectually: you understood them deeply. Concepts that in normal life sound like clichés, and there they suddenly became real.

For example, *what it really means that the journey is inside yourself*.

Or what it really means to **feel a calling**, to feel that you could dedicate your life to something and that everything else, for a moment, loses meaning.

## The things I had “already understood”

One of the strongest feelings was exactly this: I wasn’t just understanding, I was **understanding again**. As if some things had already been seen, already been known, and during the trip they came back with terrifying clarity.

I remember a moment under my gray blanket, looking at the light passing through the fabric, and this kind of joy came over me, almost emotion: **oh my God, I had already understood this**.

It was a moment of **realization**, of laughter, of crying, all at once.

Among the things I felt I was understanding was also the way we idealize people, the way you can fall in love even with someone you don’t really know. Specific examples came into my mind, faces, people, my own stories. Not as ordered thoughts, but as intuitions lighting up all at once.

And inside my head there were also these external fragments: Reddit posts, book-like phrases, sentences like **“life is a journey,”** **“every trip is a journey,”** things you normally read and scroll past, but there they felt actually true.

## The answer is not in the drug

In the middle of all this there was a constant, very strong voice repeating: **the answer is not in the drug**. The answer is not in the mushrooms. It isn’t in the substance itself.

And that was so strange, because this voice was coming while I was there, after having decided at nine in the morning to weigh out two and a half grams of mushrooms and take them.

So I was living this absurd contradiction.

On one side I felt I was entering a place where there are real, deep, important insights.

On the other side there was this voice almost mocking me: *okay, but in the end what are you going to say?* That one Sunday morning you took two and a half grams of mushrooms.

And that also made me laugh. A lot.

Because it’s true, and at the same time it’s **reductive** in an unbearable way.

Because the experience isn’t “I took mushrooms.” The experience is **how you live everything that happens inside that**.

## Art, music, and the failure of words

One of the strongest insights of the trip was this: **music, art, poetry, photography, film** are the ways people who have been in the world of here try to tell something to the world of there.

Because the world of here isn’t rational. It doesn’t work like the normal world. And so it can’t really be told through a language that is fully rational.

It felt to me like **music** was the best way to get close to that thing. As if people who make music are among the few who have taken seriously what happened to them there and are really trying to bring something back.

At the same time, though, that idea frustrated me so much.

Because I felt that art is only a **compressed** form of that message. A song, a painting, a film: they are ways of compressing an enormous experience into something transmissible. But whoever receives it has no access to the **raw data**, to what you really lived.

The decompression algorithm isn’t enough.

The other person may grasp something, may feel a shadow of it, but they cannot **reconstruct the full experience**.

And that bothered me in an almost physical way.

## “There are no words” wasn’t enough for me

During the trip I felt this double movement very strongly.

On one side: *it’s okay like this*. It’s okay that this thing is mine. It’s okay that there is no perfect way to communicate it. Even this solitude is okay.

On the other: **it’s not enough** to say “there are no words.”

That sentence really irritated me. Because it’s true, but it’s too easy. It felt like giving up.

I felt a real need to make the effort to **describe it**, to find words, images, sounds, anything. Because otherwise the only version of all this you can tell becomes: one Sunday morning I woke up and took two and a half grams of mushrooms.

And no.

There was much more.

Even if I couldn’t grasp it well, even if it escaped me while I was trying to say it.

## The temptation to share everything

At some moments I felt almost as if the only sensible thing to do was to **get up and write**, or draw, or fix somewhere what I was living.

There was even a part of me saying: *this is it, this is the explanation of your whole life*. You have to follow yourself, you have to do something creative, you have to make music.

Not in the sense that a trip magically gives you a final answer. But I deeply understood that feeling by which someone decides to dedicate their life to one thing, like music, because they feel there is a real thread there to follow.

And I also understood why there are people or places built around these substances, mushrooms, ayahuasca, the desire to bring others into that world. Because if you feel there are important things there, then you start thinking: *what if we all went there together?* What if, by sharing it, we could take one step further?

I even imagined a form of communication that doesn’t exist yet. Something that isn’t words, images, or music. A technology, or at least a way of **capturing the state of the brain** in a given instant and transferring it to another person, so they could live exactly what you are living.

That would be incredible.

Maybe it’s impossible. But during the trip it felt like the most obvious thing in the world to want.

## Being alone, and laughing anyway

At a certain point I also felt very strongly the **solitude** of all this.

If what exists in the world of here cannot really be transmitted, then all the people who got there before me must have lived the exact same frustration. They understood huge things, maybe the most important things, and then couldn’t really bring them back into the normal world.

That idea made me feel bad and at the same time made me laugh.

There was a kind of cosmic irony in it.

As if to say: maybe the answers are there, maybe many people pass through it, maybe many people understand something… and yet then you come back here and all you can do is try, badly, to tell it.

So yes, **crying, laughter, tears**, all together.

And also a form of acceptance: *we’re alone, but it’s okay like that*.

## The strangest and slightly scary part

Then there was a second part of the trip that was stranger, almost more negative.

I was still conscious. I could open my eyes, look at my phone, scroll, watch videos. I knew I was in reality. But at the same time my head kept taking off on its own in absurd ways.

All it took was closing my eyes, or shifting my attention for a second, and another completely nonsensical journey would begin.

At one point there was a reality where **we were all mice**. I don’t know why. Maybe I was a blind mouse, or there were blind mice. Meanwhile I had Bad Bunny in my headphones and maybe I was even scrolling TikTok. Completely random.

And it was also a little scary, because there was that feeling: *okay, but am I coming back to normal?*

I wasn’t gone from the world. I was there, alert. But my brain kept making these jumps, these strange flights. And every now and then there was that panic like: *am I going crazy? will I stay like this?*

Then maybe right after that I’d tell myself: *this is okay too*. Or: *it’s okay even if it means nothing*.

## The day after, normality

Another strong thought was the return. The fact that after something this intense I would still end up back at home, in normal life, and that **tomorrow maybe nothing would have changed**.

There was something hard about that.

But at the same time I was also thinking: *it depends on me*. It depends on me whether I want to make something of this experience. And together with that, the other voice again: *but it’s also okay if you make nothing of it*.

It’s okay even if the meaning of all this was simply that on a Sunday morning I had the time, the space, and the chance to take mushrooms and live this thing.

It’s not mandatory to extract a moral from it.

It’s not mandatory to find a final explanation.

## What remains

What remains for me more than anything is this constant tension between two truths.

The first is that **some things can’t really be said**.

The second is that **it is still worth trying**.

What remains is the idea of **here and there**, the very strong sense of having already been there, of having already understood certain things and then forgotten them again.

What remains is that voice saying: **the answer is not in the substance**.

What remains is the sense that art, music, the desire to create and share may also be born from here: from the stubborn attempt to bring back something from a place that refuses translation.

And what remains is a question, maybe the simplest and biggest one: if so many people, in different ways, feel they live similar insights, receive similar messages, arrive at similar phrases — *life is a journey, the trip is a journey, the answer is not in the substance* — then **is there really something in common that we all touch?**

I don’t know.

I only know that for a few hours it felt like I had entered a real place, already familiar, impossible to explain properly.

And that today, the day after, this is the most honest way I’ve found to tell it.
